    What this course is about

    CSCI 2244 provides an introduction to fundamental concepts and tools that enable advanced work in modeling and computation involving randomness. The focus is on discrete probability. Topics include probability spaces, random variables, conditional probability, limit theorems, and Markov chains. Selected topics in linear algebra are discussed in connection with the analysis of covariance. Randomness in the sense of algorithmic succinctness (e.g., Kolmogorov complexity) is mentioned in passing, but is not discussed at length. Computational illustrations in Python are given throughout the course.

    Prerequisites / Required Background

    The official prerequisites for CSCI 2244 are Computer Science I (CSCI 1101) and calculus (MATH 1102/1103 or equivalent). In particular, students entering CSCI 2244 should be comfortable with fundamental programming techniques, as well as with basic concepts of differential and integral calculus. Writing short programs in Python will be required in some of the problem sets. Please install Python 3.9 or higher, if you don't already have it.

    By the way, if your CS1 course did not use Python as the programming language, don't panic. Python is easy to learn, and you won't need all of its features right away. See https://docs.python.org/tutorial/ to get started.

    How to do well in this course

    This is a challenging course. You should plan to work hard, to keep up with the reading assignments, and to start problem sets early. Engage with the course material, in class and out.

    Assigned reading will usually follow the in-lecture discussion fairly closely, but there may be a few exceptions to this rule. Therefore, it is important to make sure that you've understood all of the the material covered in the lectures in addition to that in the reading assignments. I cannot overstate the importance of conscientiously doing all of the problem sets in order to master the course material. When preparing problem set solutions, you should strive for maximum clarity. Your write-ups should reveal the key ingredients and the structure of your reasoning. Include detailed explanations. These comments apply equally to exam solutions.

    I'll be happy to discuss any questions during my regular office hours, and encourage you to attend our TAs' office hours, as well. If you can't make it to my office hours, please contact me by e-mail so that we can try to schedule an appointment at a mutually convenient time.

    Grading

    The course grade will be based on problem sets, exams, and participation.

    • Problem sets will be due on Canvas most Fridays (but check each problem set statement for possible exceptions). You must submit at least six problem sets that demonstrate a serious attempt at completion in order to be eligible for a passing grade in the course. Each problem set will be posted approximately one week before the due date.

      Late submissions will receive no credit except in very unusual circumstances (at the discretion of the instructor). On the other hand, I will drop your lowest homework grade, such as a zero from a missed assignment, no questions asked. The average score on the problem sets will contribute 10% of your course grade. More importantly, however, your work on the problem sets will be crucial in developing a mastery of the material. Think twice before deciding to skip a problem set or not do it fully, as this would negatively impact your performance on exams.

    • Three exams will be given before the end of classes (see the schedule for dates). The exams will cover material from class, from the problem sets, and from the book. You may use your own cheat sheet (single-sided sheet of letter-sized paper of your own notes) during exams. No sharing of materials or information is allowed. The average of each student's exam scores will contribute 80% of their course grade.

      Brief quizzes may also be given during some class periods, possibly without prior notice. The overall quiz score will count as 10% of the course grade.

      There will be no make-up exams or quizzes. In order to allow for unexpected events, I will drop each student's lowest quiz score.

      Do not be fooled by the low weight given to homework scores in the overall grade, relative to exams and quizzes. The not-so-secret recipe for doing well on the latter is to work diligently on the problem sets and to stay up to date on the course material.

    • My assessment of each student's level of participation in the course may enter into deciding the final grade in cases in which a student's overall score puts them on the boundary between two letter grades.

    Academic Honesty

    You are encouraged to discuss assigned homework problems (but not exams) with other people but you must individually design and write your own solutions for all assignments. Furthermore, you should explicitly acknowledge any sources of ideas used that are not your own; this includes other people, chatbots, books, web sites, etc. You should not discuss exam problems with anyone except the instructor. Please also read the Boston College academic integrity policy. Behavior that is not in the spirit of the above guidelines may be reported to the appropriate class dean for review by the Academic Integrity Board. If you're in doubt about what constitutes academic dishonesty in a particular situation, talk to me.
