    What this course is about

    This course provides an introduction to concepts and techniques of computational data modeling and inference that can inform rational and responsible decision-making based on data. Topics include data preprocessing, exploratory data analysis and visualization, predictive and descriptive modeling based on statistical and machine learning concepts and approaches, and matters related to bias, confidentiality, and privacy. Programming in Python will be required. Prospective students should be comfortable with mathematical notation and reasoning at the college calculus level, as well as basic concepts of mathematical probability.

    Necessary Background

     As of the 2023-2024 academic year, CSCI 2291 is a core course in the MCAS Data Science Minor. The official prerequisites are CSCI 1090 Data Science Principles and MATH 2250 Mathematical Foundations of Data Science. In particular, it is assumed that students will be familiar with the basics of the following topics before starting CSCI 2291:

    • Computational problem-solving, program design in Python, including procedural abstraction / decomposition, functions, conditional branching, iteration, list comprehension, and recursion.

    • General mathematical notation and reasoning at the level of college calculus, including summation notation, algebraic reasoning, functions, and differential and integral calculus with functions of one or more variables.

    • Basics of matrices and matrix arithmetic.

    • Concepts of probability, including sample spaces, probability distributions, random variables, independence and conditional probability, and jointly distributed random variables.

    Alternatively, the needed programming background for this course can be acquired through CSCI 1101 (Computer Science I) or an equivalent introduction to Computer Science and programming. The minimum alternative mathematical background includes MATH 1101, 1103, 1005, or an equivalent course in college-level calculus, and probability at the level of CSCI 2244 Randomness & Computation or MATH 4410 Probability.

    Please note, however, that for purposes of the Data Science Minor, any substitutions for CSCI 1090 and MATH 2250 would need to be approved by the Data Science Program Director.

    How to do well in this course

    Take charge

    As a 2000-level course, CSCI 2291 requires greater autonomy from students than do typical 1000-level courses. Some of the material in the course is intellectually challenging. Lectures will cover most of the main ideas, but you will need to fill in the gaps by studying the references and other materials linked from the course homepage, as well as the software documentation. Complete the reading assignments conscientiously. A first reading is advisable before class.

    Tune out the noise

    Engage fully with the material, in class and out. I'll help with this by not allowing the use of laptops or phones during class, except that laptops will be needed for quizzes. Phones may be used in emergencies, of course. I strongly encourage you to adopt a similar policy when you are studying and when designing programs (except for the actual implementation phase).

    Start early

    Get started on each homework assignment as soon as possible. Plan to go over your own notes and over the relevant course materials several times in connection with a specific concept or problem. This process may take several hours or even days, depending on the complexity of the issue at hand.

    Explain your thinking

    Strive for maximum clarity and simplicity when preparing homework and quiz solutions. I'm looking for lucid, step-by-step explanations and discussion, not just the final answers. Aim for clarity over length.

    Grading

    The course grade will be based on homework, quizzes and active course involvement, and a final exam.

    Late homework submissions will receive no credit

    (except, possibly, in very unusual circumstances, at the discretion of the instructor). As students in my previous courses can confirm, I mean this, so please plan ahead. On the other hand,

    "One free strike" policy

    I will drop each student's lowest homework score and lowest quiz score automatically, no questions asked. I highly recommend that you not elect to miss a homework or quiz unless the circumstances merit it, as you may need the break unexpectedly later in the semester.

    • Homework (20%)

      Homework assignments will be posted on Canvas during the semester. Due dates will be posted in the schedule section of the CSCI 2291 homepage. Each student's lowest homework grade will be dropped.

    • Quizzes and course involvement (50%)

      Several quizzes will be given during the term. Students should bring a laptop computer to class on which they can access Canvas. The quizzes may cover material from class, from the posted reading materials, or from the homework assignments. My "one free strike" policy applies to both homework and quizzes: makeup quizzes will not be offered, but I will drop each student's lowest quiz score automatically, no questions asked.

      Active involvement in course-related activities is required. A substantial degree of independent work outside of class is expected, including completing the assigned reading before class (see the Schedule section on this page). Course involvement may play a role in determining final grades in cases in which the total score is on the border between letter categories.

    • Group term project (30%)

      Groups of students will collaborate on a substantial project that illustrates key data science themes, developing some aspects further than in class. Projects can focus on a particular application, a set of techniques, or a data science issue ranging from technical processing steps to societal impact. The project will require focused work over much of the semester. Deliverables will include a presentation and a written report.

    Academic Honesty

    You must individually design and write your own solutions for all assignments unless otherwise stated. Furthermore, you should explicitly acknowledge any sources of ideas that are not your own. This includes other people, books, web pages, etc. Behavior that is not in the spirit of these guidelines is considered to be academically dishonest and may be reported to the appropriate class dean for review by the Academic Integrity Board. If you're unsure about what constitutes academic dishonesty in a particular situation, ask me before proceeding further.

    You may also consult the university's academic integrity policies.
