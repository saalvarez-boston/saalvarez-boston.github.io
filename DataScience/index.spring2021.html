    What this course is about

    This course provides an introduction to concepts and techniques of computational data modeling and inference that can inform rational decision-making based on data. Topics include data preprocessing, exploratory data analysis and visualization, elements of probability and statistical inference, and predictive and descriptive modeling, with an introduction to machine learning concepts and approaches as time allows. Programming in Python will be required. Prospective students should also be comfortable with mathematical notation and reasoning at the college calculus level.

    Necessary Background

     CSCI 2291 has as prerequisites the course CSCI 1101 (Computer Science I) or an equivalent introduction to Computer Science and programming, and one of MATH 1101, 1103, 1005, or an equivalent course in college-level calculus. In particular, it is assumed that students will be familiar with the basics of the following topics before starting CSCI 2291:

    • General mathematical notation and reasoning at the level of college calculus, including summation notation, algebraic reasoning, functions, and differential and integral calculus with functions of one variable.

    • Computational problem-solving, program design in Python, including procedural abstraction / decomposition, functions, conditional branching, iteration, list comprehension, and recursion.

    Note: Python is a comparatively easy language to learn. A student who has taken an introduction to Computer Science using a language other than Python should be able to learn the required Python skills with a modest amount of additional study.

    How to do well in this course

    Take charge

    As a 2000-level course, CSCI 2291 requires greater autonomy from students than do typical 1000-level courses. Some of the material in the course is intellectually challenging. Lectures will cover most of the main ideas, but you will need to fill in the gaps by studying the references and other materials linked from the course homepage, as well as the software documentation. Complete the reading assignments conscientiously. A first reading is advisable before class.

    Tune out the noise

    Engage fully with the material, in class and out. I'll help with this by not allowing the use of laptops or phones during class, except that laptops will be needed for quizzes. Phones may be used in emergencies, of course. I strongly encourage you to adopt a similar policy when you are studying and outlining program designs, except for the actual implementation phase.

    Start early

    Get started on each homework assignment as soon as possible. Plan to go over your own notes and over the relevant course materials several times in connection with a specific concept or problem. This process may take several hours or even days, depending on the complexity of the issue at hand.

    Explain your thinking

    Strive for maximum clarity and simplicity when preparing homework and quiz solutions. I'm looking for lucid, step-by-step explanations and discussion, not just the final answers. Aim for clarity over length.

    Grading

    The course grade will be based on quizzes and active course involvement, and homework. While the class will meet in person when health conditions allow, attendance is not required. Priority should be given to health considerations. If you are not feeling well, please do not come to class. Lectures will be captured on video and posted on the course Canvas site later on the same day.

    • Homework (50%)

      Homework assignments will be posted on Canvas during the semester. Due dates will be posted in the schedule section of the CSCI 2291 homepage. Late homework submissions will receive no credit, except, possibly, in very unusual circumstances, at the discretion of the instructor. As students in my previous courses can confirm, I mean this, so please plan ahead. On the other hand, I will drop each student's lowest homework score automatically, no questions asked. This is my "one free strike" policy.

    • Quizzes and course involvement (50%)

      Active involvement in coure-related activities is required. A substantial degree of independent work outside of class is expected, including completing the assigned reading before class (see the Schedule section on this page). Several online quizzes will be given during the term. The quizzes may cover material from class, from the posted reading materials, or from the homework assignments.

      Quizzes will require the use of a laptop, so bring yours to class if you will be in attendance on a given day, minding the above policy restricting the in-class use of laptops at other times. My "one free strike" policy also applies to quizzes: makeup quizzes will not be offered, but I will drop each student's lowest quiz score automatically, no questions asked.

    Academic Honesty

    You must individually design and write your own solutions for all assignments unless otherwise stated. Furthermore, you should explicitly acknowledge any sources of ideas that are not your own. This includes other people, books, web pages, etc. Behavior that is not in the spirit of these guidelines is considered to be academically dishonest and may be reported to the appropriate class dean for review by the Academic Integrity Board. If you're unsure about what constitutes academic dishonesty in a particular situation, ask me before proceeding further.

    You may also consult the university's academic integrity policies.
